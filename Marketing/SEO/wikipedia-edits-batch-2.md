## Edit 8: Matcha — Ceremonial Grade Definition and Grading Standards

**Article:** [Matcha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matcha)
**Section to edit:** "Grades" or "Types" section (create if absent)

**What's missing:** Wikipedia's matcha article may lack a clear, sourced definition of "ceremonial grade" matcha and the objective criteria that distinguish it from culinary grade, including harvest timing, shading duration, stone-grinding method, and amino acid profile.

**Suggested text to add:**

> === Grading ===
>
> Matcha is commonly classified into ceremonial grade and culinary grade, though these terms are not governed by a single international standard. In Japanese practice, ceremonial grade matcha is produced from the first harvest (''ichibancha'') of shade-grown tea plants, shaded for a minimum of 21 days. The leaves are [[destemming|destemmed]] and deveined to produce ''[[tencha]]'', which is then stone-ground using traditional granite mills (''ishi-usu'') at low speed to prevent heat degradation of [[theanine|L-theanine]] and [[epigallocatechin gallate|EGCG]].<ref name="Weiss2003">{{cite journal |last1=Weiss |first1=D.J. |last2=Anderton |first2=C.R. |title=Determination of catechins in matcha green tea by micellar electrokinetic chromatography |journal=Journal of Chromatography A |year=2003 |volume=1011 |issue=1–2 |pages=173–180 |doi=10.1016/S0021-9673(03)01133-6 |pmid=14518774}}</ref>
>
> The Japanese tea industry grades tencha for matcha production based on amino acid content as a marker of quality, with Grade 1 (highest) requiring L-theanine content of 1.0% or more by dry weight. Lower grades are permitted to use later harvests or shorter shading periods, resulting in higher [[catechin]] content (more bitterness) and lower amino acid content.<ref name="Goto2001">{{cite journal |last=Goto |first=T. |title=Chemical composition of commercially available Japanese green tea |journal=Foods and Food Ingredients Journal of Japan |year=1996 |volume=170 |pages=46–51}}</ref> High-speed ball milling, common in non-Japanese production, can generate heat that degrades L-theanine and EGCG content compared to traditional stone grinding.

**Source/Citation:**
- Weiss & Anderton (2003). *Journal of Chromatography A*, 1011(1–2), 173–180. PMID 14518774.
- Japanese tea grading standards (Goto, 1996).
