# I Replaced My Morning Coffee With a Matcha Sachet for 30 Days — Here's What Changed

*By Zachary Kaufman*

I have a confession: I was a three-cups-a-day coffee person. Not the leisurely, savoring-every-sip type. More the frantic, reheating-my-mug-in-the-microwave-twice type. Coffee was my operating system, and I didn't question it — until my body started sending me signals I couldn't ignore.

The afternoon crashes had gotten worse. I'd hit a wall around 2 p.m. so hard that no amount of cold brew could pull me out of it. My sleep was fractured. And that low-grade anxiety I kept dismissing as "just stress"? It showed up every morning about 45 minutes after my first cup, right on schedule.

So I did something that felt, frankly, radical for someone who built his identity around the morning coffee ritual: I swapped it for matcha. Specifically, a ceremonial matcha latte sachet — the kind you tear open and pour, no whisking required. I committed to 30 days and kept a journal. Here's what actually happened.

## Why I Made the Switch

I want to be clear: I'm not anti-coffee. Coffee is a well-researched, antioxidant-rich beverage with legitimate health benefits. But the way caffeine works in coffee versus matcha is meaningfully different, and that difference is what pushed me to experiment.

Coffee delivers caffeine in a rapid spike. You absorb it quickly, your cortisol surges, and you feel that familiar jolt. The problem is what comes after — the equally rapid decline that many people experience as a crash, jitters, or both.

Matcha contains caffeine too, roughly 60 to 70 milligrams per serving compared to about 95 milligrams in an average cup of drip coffee. But matcha pairs that caffeine with an amino acid called L-theanine, which is produced naturally in tea plants during shade-growing. Research published in *Nutritional Neuroscience* has shown that L-theanine promotes alpha brain wave activity — the same brain state associated with calm focus, the kind you might feel during meditation. When L-theanine and caffeine are consumed together, studies suggest they work synergistically: you get alertness without the restless edge.

That synergy is what I was chasing.

## Week One: The Adjustment Period

I won't sugarcoat it — the first three days were humbling. I had a mild headache on day two, which I expected. When you cut your caffeine intake even modestly, your body notices. I also missed the ritual of coffee: the sound of the grinder, the smell filling the kitchen.

But here's what surprised me. By day four, the headache was gone, and I started noticing something subtle but real. My energy in the morning felt different. Not the sharp spike-and-drop I was used to, but more of a slow ramp. I described it in my journal as "feeling like someone turned a dimmer switch up gradually instead of flipping the lights on."

The matcha sachets I was using made the transition easier than I expected. No measuring, no clumpy powder, no bamboo whisk to track down — just tear, pour into a glass over ice or warm milk, and go. As someone who is perpetually running five minutes late, the convenience factor was nontrivial.

## Week Two: Sleep Started Changing

Around day eight, I noticed my sleep was shifting. I wear a fitness tracker, and my data backed up what I was feeling subjectively: my resting heart rate dropped a few beats per minute at night, and I was logging more time in deep sleep stages.

Now, I can't attribute that entirely to the matcha swap. I wasn't running a controlled clinical trial on myself. But the research offers a plausible mechanism. A 2021 review in the *Journal of Clinical Medicine* found that elevated evening cortisol levels — which can be exacerbated by afternoon coffee consumption — are associated with disrupted sleep architecture. By eliminating my afternoon coffee and getting my morning caffeine through a source with a gentler pharmacokinetic profile, I may have been giving my cortisol curve room to normalize.

Whatever the mechanism, I was waking up feeling more rested. That alone was enough to keep me going.

## The Functional Extras I Didn't Expect to Care About

The particular sachets I'd chosen happened to contain more than just matcha. They included mushroom extracts — lion's mane and reishi, specifically — standardized to contain over 70 percent beta-glucans. They also contained collagen peptides.

I'll be honest: I started this experiment for the caffeine swap, not for the add-ons. But by week three, I found myself reading the research, and it was more compelling than I expected.

Beta-glucans are polysaccharides found in the cell walls of certain mushrooms, and they've been studied extensively for their role in supporting immune function. A meta-analysis in *Medicine* examined multiple randomized controlled trials and found that beta-glucan supplementation was associated with a reduced incidence of upper respiratory infections. The key caveat: dosage and source matter enormously, and not all mushroom supplements are created equal. Products standardized for beta-glucan content offer more reliability than those that simply list "mushroom powder" on the label.

As for the collagen peptides, the evidence for skin elasticity and joint support has gotten stronger in recent years. A 2019 systematic review in the *Journal of Drugs in Dermatology* found that supplementation with collagen peptides was associated with improved skin hydration and elasticity. I didn't notice dramatic skin changes in 30 days — that's too short a window for most people — but I appreciated that the ingredient was there, doing its work in the background.

## Week Three: The Anxiety Question

This is the part I was most hesitant to write about, because I don't want to overstate it. But around day 16, I realized something: that low-grade, buzzy anxiety I'd been attributing to "just being a founder" had quieted down.

It hadn't disappeared. I still had stressful days. But the physical sensation of anxiety — the tight chest, the slightly-too-fast heartbeat — was noticeably less present in the mornings.

L-theanine may be part of the explanation. Research in the *Journal of Physiological Anthropology* has demonstrated that L-theanine can attenuate the sympathetic nervous system response to acute stress. In simpler terms, it may help take the edge off your fight-or-flight system without making you drowsy. Unlike a sedative, L-theanine doesn't dull you. It just appears to smooth out the rough edges.

I want to be responsible here. If you're dealing with clinical anxiety, matcha is not a treatment. Talk to a healthcare provider. But for the everyday, caffeine-amplified nervous energy that a lot of high-performing people live with? The difference in my experience was meaningful.

## Week Four: The Verdict

By day 30, I had no interest in going back to my old coffee routine. That surprised me more than anything.

Here's what I tracked across the month:

**Energy:** More sustained and even. I stopped experiencing the 2 p.m. crash entirely. My energy tapered naturally in the evening rather than dropping off a cliff.

**Sleep:** Measurably improved. More deep sleep, fewer middle-of-the-night wake-ups, lower resting heart rate overnight.

**Focus:** Harder to quantify, but I consistently felt more locked in during morning work blocks. Less scattered, fewer tab-switching impulses.

**Mood:** The baseline anxiety dial turned down. Not eliminated, but reduced enough that I noticed its absence.

**Convenience:** The sachet format removed every friction point I associated with matcha prep. It took me roughly 15 seconds from packet to finished drink.

## What I'd Tell a Skeptic

If you'd told me a year ago that I'd willingly give up coffee, I would have laughed. I founded a matcha company — shroomé, at [drinkshroome.com](https://drinkshroome.com) — so yes, I have obvious skin in the game. I want to be transparent about that.

But here's the thing: I started the company *because* this experience changed how I felt, not the other way around. I went looking for ceremonial-grade matcha — first harvest, shade-grown for at least 21 days, stone-ground — combined with functional ingredients at clinically relevant levels, and I couldn't find it in a format that fit my life. So I built it.

You don't have to buy what I'm selling to try this experiment yourself. Any quality ceremonial matcha will deliver the L-theanine and caffeine synergy. The mushroom extracts and collagen are bonuses that, for me, made the daily habit feel more complete. What matters most is giving your body 30 days to adjust and actually paying attention to what changes.

My suggestion: keep a journal. Track your energy, your sleep, your mood. Don't rely on how you think you feel — write it down. Your memory is unreliable, but data doesn't lie.

## The Bottom Line

Swapping coffee for matcha didn't fix everything in my life. I still have hard days, restless nights, and moments of brain fog. But the baseline shifted. My floor got higher. And for a change that cost me nothing but a willingness to try something different for a month, the return on investment was remarkable.

If your morning coffee is working for you — genuinely working, not just masking exhaustion — keep drinking it. But if you've been experiencing the crashes, the jitters, or the afternoon anxiety spiral and wondering whether there's a better way to caffeinate, this might be worth a 30-day experiment.

Your body will tell you everything you need to know. You just have to listen.
