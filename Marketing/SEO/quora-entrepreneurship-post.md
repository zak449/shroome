## Version 1: "What advice would you give to a first-time CPG founder?"

I'm about a year into building my first CPG brand — a functional matcha latte called shroomé — and honestly, the advice I'd give my past self looks nothing like the advice I see in most startup threads. So here's what I've actually learned, not the stuff that sounds good on a podcast.

**Your product decisions will haunt you or save you.** We spent months debating liquid sachets vs. powder tubs. Every mentor told us to go with powder — it's cheaper to produce, easier to ship, longer shelf life. But I kept coming back to the fact that nobody wants to measure scoops and whisk matcha at 7am on a Tuesday. We went with liquid sachets. Tear, pour, done. It costs us more per unit and our margins are tighter because of it. But the format IS the product for our customer. If we had launched as another powder in a jar, we'd be competing on price with brands that have ten years of distribution on us. The sachet is our moat, even though it makes everything harder operationally.

**Ingredient sourcing will break your brain.** We use ceremonial-grade matcha, fruiting body mushroom extracts, and grass-fed collagen. Sounds simple on a label. In reality, finding a mushroom extract supplier who could verify 70%+ beta-glucan concentration through third-party testing took months. Most suppliers were selling mycelium grown on rice and calling it "mushroom extract." The beta-glucan numbers they quoted were inflated because basic tests can't distinguish mushroom beta-glucans from grain starch alpha-glucans. We went through four suppliers before finding one that could provide legitimate fruiting body extracts with real Megazyme testing. For the matcha, we needed first-harvest, shade-grown minimum 21 days — and we needed it in a format that worked for liquid sachets, not just traditional whisking. Every single ingredient had a sourcing story like this.

**Go DTC first, even if retail is the dream.** We chose direct-to-consumer for launch and I'm glad we did. Retail sounds sexy — get into Erewhon, get into Whole Foods, instant credibility. But the economics are brutal for a first-time founder. Retail margins eat 40-60% of your revenue. You need slotting fees, broker relationships, distributor agreements, and enough capital to absorb chargebacks and spoilage. DTC lets you own the customer relationship, test pricing, iterate on messaging, and actually learn what your customer cares about before you hand over control to a buyer who gives you 30 seconds to pitch.

**Transparency is a competitive advantage, but it's also terrifying.** We publish every ingredient and its exact amount — no proprietary blends. That means anyone can look at our formula, compare it to published research, and tell us if our dosing is wrong. That vulnerability is uncomfortable. But it also builds a type of trust that "proprietary blend" brands can never access. Our customer reads labels. They're skeptical by default. The only way to earn their trust is to give them everything and let them decide.

If I could go back, I'd tell myself: pick the hard road on product quality, pick the easy road on everything else. Don't over-engineer your website. Don't obsess over logo fonts. Obsess over what's inside the sachet.

We're still pre-launch, building our waitlist and figuring out a lot of this in real time. If you're curious what we've built so far, I'd genuinely appreciate honest feedback — drinkshroome.com. Tell me what works, what doesn't, what's confusing. First-time founders need real input more than they need encouragement.

## Version 2: "What's the hardest part about launching a beverage brand?"

I'm in the middle of launching a functional matcha latte brand called shroomé, and I can tell you the hardest part isn't what most people think. It's not coming up with the idea. It's not the branding. It's the thousand invisible decisions that each narrow your path and determine whether you survive or don't.

**Supply chain for specialty ingredients is a nightmare.** Our product has three core ingredients: ceremonial-grade matcha, organic mushroom extracts, and grass-fed collagen peptides. Each one has its own sourcing hell.

Ceremonial matcha has to be first-harvest, shade-grown for at least 21 days, stone-ground. Real ceremonial grade costs $0.75-$1.50 per gram. We're putting 2g in each sachet, so matcha alone is $1.50-$3.00 of our COGS before we even touch the other ingredients. We went through multiple suppliers and sample rounds before locking in a source that hit our quality spec at a price that didn't destroy our unit economics.

Mushroom extracts were worse. We needed fruiting body extracts — not mycelium grown on grain — with verified beta-glucan concentration above 70%. Most US-market mushroom supplements test at 15-30% beta-glucans, and a lot of that is actually alpha-glucan from the rice substrate, not real mushroom-derived compounds. We needed a supplier who could provide Megazyme-verified beta-glucan testing on every batch. Four suppliers didn't make the cut before we found one who could actually deliver.

Collagen sourcing was the "easiest" of the three, and it still took weeks. Grass-fed, pasture-raised bovine, hydrolyzed to the right molecular weight for bioavailability, Type I and Type III. The supply chain for this is more established, but verifying grass-fed claims requires auditing documentation that some suppliers don't want to provide.

**The retail vs. DTC decision will define your first two years.** Everyone told me to get into retail — Erewhon, Whole Foods, specialty grocers. The prestige is real. But here's the math nobody tells first-time founders: retail margins are 40-60%. Add slotting fees ($5,000-$25,000 per SKU at major retailers), broker commissions (typically 5-7%), distributor markups (25-35%), and the cost of in-store demos and promotional spend. You can easily burn through $50,000-$100,000 just to sit on a shelf for six months and hope your velocity is high enough to avoid getting pulled.

We went DTC. Our margins are better, we own the customer email and data, and we can iterate on pricing and messaging in real time. The tradeoff is that customer acquisition cost through paid social is expensive and rising. We're betting on organic content and community to bring that CAC down over time rather than writing checks to Meta.

**Pricing strategy is where your identity lives.** We sell a 12-sachet box. Each sachet replaces a $5-7 cafe matcha latte. Our price point needs to feel like a deal compared to the cafe but premium enough to signal quality. Price too low and people assume your ceremonial matcha is actually culinary grade and your mushroom extracts are filler. Price too high and you're competing with luxury brands that have established trust and distribution. We landed on a price that puts each sachet around $3-4, which positions us as "better than the cafe, fraction of the cost" without looking cheap. Whether that's right, I honestly don't know yet.

**GMP certification, third-party testing, and regulatory compliance are invisible costs that add up fast.** Every batch gets tested for heavy metals — arsenic, lead, cadmium, mercury. Every batch gets microbial testing. We manufacture in a GMP-certified facility. None of this shows up in a flashy Instagram post, but it's thousands of dollars per production run that you have to build into your model from day one.

The hardest part of launching a beverage brand isn't any single thing. It's that every decision is interconnected. Your ingredient sourcing affects your COGS, which affects your pricing, which affects your channel strategy, which affects your marketing, which affects your unit economics. Pull one thread and everything moves.

I'd love honest feedback on what we've built so far — drinkshroome.com
